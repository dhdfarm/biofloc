The Functions of Actinobacteria in Biofloc

The relative abundance of Actinobacteria increased in water and fish intestines in BFTs, demonstrating their importance to the formation and consumption of biofloc.

Actinobacteria are Gram-positive, saprophytic bacteria which often grow extensive mycelia. Their extensive capability to produce antibiotics may serve to control pathogens in aquaculture (Goodfellow and Fiedler, 2010), which was corroborated by the strong decline in relative abundance of Aeromonas sp. in BTF treatments.

Aeromonas sp. dominated in the intestines of fish in CT with mean proportion of 20%. However, Aeromonas sp. was absent from the intestines of fish in BT and BZT. Actinobacteria are also as sources of earth off-flavor chemicals, geosmin and 2-MIB, in aquaculture (Gerber and Lechevalier, 1965; Schrader and Summerfelt, 2010).

Cyanobacteria are commonly considered the major contributors of these off-flavor episodes in aquaculture ponds, the relative abundance of Cyanobacteria was diminished in BT and BZT, while geosmin and 2-MIB concentrations increased because of Actinobacterial relative abundances.

Thus, we propose that Actinobacteria perform four major functions in biofloc aquaculture systems: 1) their filamentous structure to promote the formation of floc; 2) their antibiotics protect against fish pathogens; 3) their biomass serves a nutritive function; and 4) they produce geosmin and 2-MIB leading to occurrence of earth off-flavor in fish.
