The Formation and Structure of Biofloc

Bioaugmentation with the activate sludge flocculating bacteria, Zoogloea resiniphila MMB, had no discernable impact on biofloc formation in our aquaculture system.

There were no differences between the two biofloc treatments. Z. resiniphila MMB was not detected by 16 s RNA sequencing.

The failure of the bioaugmentation should because that glucose was not the ideal carbon source and the competition in the original pond water was excessive for Z. resiniphila MMB.

The two closest relatives to Zoogloea had low sequence similarity and were also low in abundance in the water. In contrast, Actinobacteria were dominant in the BFT treatments, and other filamentous bacterium were also significantly increased in the water environment of BFT.

These results indicated that filamentous bacterium formed the major structure of biofloc, which was different to bacterial communities in activated sludge. Floc-forming bacterial strains from activated sludge are commonly members of Zoogloea (Wang et al., 2012 and Zhang et al., 2012).

Filamentous bacteria do grow in activated sludge and can be advantageous; however, when they grow in excess amounts, extending from the floc into the bulk fluid, the settling process is impaired and foaming can occur upon aeration.

These problems did not occur in the aquaculture system, likely because large particles of flocculation are more likely to be consumed by fish.
