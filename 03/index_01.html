Biofloc Formation, Water Quality and Fish Yield

Global aquaculture production of fish reached 80.0 million tons of salable food (USD 231.6 billion), and provided 10.8 kg of fish per capita consumption in 2016 (FAO, 2018). Fresh water pond plays a major role in aquaculture production, which was the source of 47.5 million tons of finfish (FAO, 2018).

Chinese pond aquaculture produced 22.9 million tons of freshwater aquaculture production and occupied 2.8 million ha of water surface in 2016 (72% of total freshwater aquaculture production, and 43.9% of total freshwater aquaculture area) (Fisheries Administration, 2017).

The average annual growth rate of world aquaculture production during 2001–2016 was 5.8% (FAO, 2018). The expansion of aquaculture is restricted by land and water requirements. Pond aquaculture relies on high stocking density, protein-rich feed and aeration.

The development of the polyculture systems, where the different fish species occupy different niches lead to major increases in productivity (Wang et al., 2015).

The combined culturing of grass carp (Ctenopharyngodon idellus), common carp (Cyprinus carpio) and crucian carp (Carassius spp.) resulted in the production of 5.9, 3.5 and 3 million tons of each fish, respectively, in China in 2016 (Fisheries Administration, 2017).

Higher productivity also depends on higher inputs of fish feed, resulting in increased waste production which has environmental and economic costs. Of the total nitrogen input to culture ponds, only 20–30% is retained in fish biomass and the rest becomes a polluting agent of the water by producing high level toxicants such as ammonia and nitrite (Islam, 2005).

As a result, it is necessary to frequently exchange pond water, leading to eutrophication and the degradation of surrounding watersheds. The accumulation of excess nitrogen also necessitates drying the pond to oxidize the organic matter accumulated as sediment or remove the sediment of the pond every year.

A more environmentally sustainable form of aquaculture, ‘Biofloc Technology’ (BFT), was first developed in the early 1970s by Aquacop (1975) at Ifremer-COP. The system was later improved by supplying carbohydrates to the system by Avnimelech in 1999.

BFT is based on encouraging the growth of microorganisms in the aquaculture by adding carbohydrates (sucrose, glucose, cassava or cellulose) to the water to control the carbon-to‑nitrogen ratio (C/N ratio), optimized for minimum water exchange (Crab et al., 2012).

High C/N ratios shift the dominant community of microorganisms from autotrophic to heterotrophic in the culturing system (Browdy et al., 2001). This promotes rapid growth of bacteria and nitrogen uptake into microbial biomass.

Flocs form from bacterial biomass, becoming more visible to fish and shrimp. Management of a BFT system minimizes the release of water from the aquaculture system, thereby reducing environmental impacts in the form of excess nutrients and pathogens (Emerenciano et al., 2013).

Today, BFT is used throughout the world with many aquaculture species. The underlying mechanisms of BFT remains unclear. The exploration of the bacterial community of aquaculture ponds and the intestines of cultured organisms is necessary to understand the mechanisms.

Cardona et al. (2016) reported that the bacterial communities in the intestines of shrimp were characteristic of the bacteria in floc, suggesting that supporting certain bacteria with favored substrate or nitrogen sources may facilitate flocculation.

In one study, the predominant flocculating bacteria was Bacillus sp. in the bioflocs treatment (Zhao et al., 2012). Ray et al. (2010) used gas chromatographic measurement of bacteria-specific fatty acids to predict the changes in bacterial abundance.

In the activated sludge (AS) process, which has been widely studied and applied for municipal sewage and industrial wastewater treatment, bacterial floc formation plays a central role in it.

A zoogloea-forming organism was isolated by An et al. (2016) from activated sludge in the Wuhan, China and identified as Zoogloea resiniphila MMB.

The addition of selected strains of microorganisms with known capabilities, or bioaugmentation, is commonly used to improve the performance of biological wastewater treatment plants (Abeysinghe et al., 2002).

Z. resiniphila MMB should have a potential ability to promoted floc formation and be used in the bioaugmentation of activated sludge.

The bacterial floc formation should be important in BFT aquaculture system. To the best of our knowledge, no study has yet examined the effect of bioaugmentation on biofloc formation in aquaculture.

In freshwater environments, water quality and fish biomass are regularly affected by earth off-flavor. The odorous compounds have been identified as geosmin and 2-methylisoborneol (2-MIB), which are semi-volatile terpenoids produced by cyanobacteria, actinomycetes, fungi, and myxobacteria (Zuo et al., 2010).

The effects of BFT on organisms which produce these compounds, and the off-flavor of fish, are not presently known. We investigated the effects of BFT and bioaugmented BFT on the water quality, nitrogen budget and generation of off-flavor in a polyculture of Chinese carp.

We traced nitrogen utilization and characterized the microbial communities in pond water and the intestines of carp in different culturing systems, namely conventional treatment (CT), biofloc-stimulated treatment (BT), and biofloc bioaugmented with Zoogloea resiniphila MMB treatment (BZT).
